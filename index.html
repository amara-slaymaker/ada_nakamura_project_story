Evolution of Agriculture
Inputs, Environmental Impacts & Outputs

World population is growing as never before and it is no secret. This increase has been made possible thanks to the progress of agricultural technologies. Modern agriculture utilizes useful tools such as machinery, fertilizers, chemical pesticides. This has made is possible for humans to harvest larger areas, increase efficiency, deal with illnesses and parasites, keep improve food shelf life. These revolutions have drastically changed agricultural practices.


Progresses often comes with drawbacks - modern agriculture is not exempt

One drawback is the increase in greenhouse gas emissions. On the following graph, one can see the evolution of the global population (in terms of number of people) and the evolution of gas emissions due to agriculture (in terms of GgCO2).

We can see that greenhouse gas emissions due to agriculture are roughly increasing collinearly with the global population until now as agriculture intensifies to keep feeding the increasing number of people. This brings concern for the future, as we are already seeing the environmental consequences of this way of life.

Back to the last decades

Let’s focus on the crops production. For those products, the FAO database proposes a consistent measure, which is the ratio between the production of a country and its harvested area. We will refer to this measure as the yield of a country. The following map represents the yield of cereals all over the world between 1961 and 2017. The countries that are displayed in grey are those for which no value is present in the FAO dataset.


A yield explosion

Those plots are more than convincing: the average yield of the top 10 countries has risen up by 3 between 1961 and 2017. This proves that the 3rd agricultural revolution did improve the yields. Moreover, the top 10 is composed of different countries in 1961 and in 2017, and a lot of those countries are rather small, like the islands of Saint Vincent and the Grenadines, Belgium or Oman. The USA are only ninth! Of course, a big production isn’t correlated with a big yield, and in 2017, the USA production of cereals is 4,000,000 tonnes whereas the Oman one is only 60000. But those figures show that a small country regarding its area can be more than 2 times more efficient than the USA.

This is no big surprise, the yield of produced food by area units is skyrocketing for more than 50 years. The Third Agricultural Revolution seemed to be a big success! It has drastically changed the agriculture habits, and lead to an assertion nearly as admitted as “eat your soup, you’ll grow up soon”, which is “the more fertilizer you use, the faster your crops will grow”. But is this sustainable? Probably not. Can we keep going with this method? Probably not. But does data support those intuitions? Do we need to change our mind and practices now, or do we still have time?

Fertilizers still enhanced yield at a global scale

We used a statistical method (Principal Component Analysis, see methods for more) to obtain one variable, called “Agriculture score”, that best represents all the yield of one country. We computed this value for all the countries and colorized it, a posteriori, using the fertilizer use of the country.

The relationship is pretty obvious, right? The green countries, using lower fertilizer amounts, have lower agriculture scores than the blue ones and the red ones (see methods for significance). This relationship can be modeled by a linear relationship and the slope of this curve correspond to the global effect of fertilizer on the agriculture score.

To wrap up, fertilizers enhanced yields at a global scale. Indeed, they had a crucial role in the Green revolution. So nothing crazy for now…

Do we need to change our practices?

But what if we take a look at the evolution of this effect over time (i.e. evolution of the slope of the linear relationship)? Are fertilizers still increasing yields? Or should we focus on alternative now?

Ok, now that’s interesting: while the median of agriculture score for all countries (left panel, in black) is stable, the median of the fertilizer use is increasing (right panel, in black). This results is even more visible for the high fertilizer group (in red): the agriculture score are slightly decreasing while the fertilizer uses are increasing. Isn’t that crazy? The fertilizer use is increasing, especially in countries that use already higher amounts of fertilizers by hectare, and the agriculture score aren’t increasing that much, and are slightly decreasing in high fertilizer countries.


And What about the Environment?

Besides helping food to grow, the use of fertilizers also has a significant environmental impact in terms of emissions, as was seen in the first plot of this page. These emissions use occur due to emissions of nitrous oxide when nitrogen is applied to soils. One tonne of nitrous oxide emissions is equivalent to almost 300 tonnes of CO2 emissions - therefore this has important consequences for global warming.

Due to the supposed link between fertilizers and yield, one might assume that in order to achieve high yields, high emissions are necessary or inevitable. But is this really the case? Below we will take a closer look at the link between emissions and agricultural yield, as we have done above for application of fertilizer.

Relationship between Yield and Emissions

Principle component analysis was used again to compare agriculture score to fertilizer use for all countries between 2002 and 2017. As can be seen in the plot, there is some noticeable separation between the countries with high emissions (red), medium emissions (yellow), and low emissions(green), and we can see that countries with the highest agricultural scores are all medium or high emitting countries.

However, there are also many countries which have a similar agricultural indicator score, while having significantly different emissions. From this, we can see that the link between agriculture and emissions is not as evident as one might initially believe.

Let's take another look:

To study this relationship more in depth, we again took a closer look at the evolution of agricultural score and emissions over the years per country. This time the classification (high, medium, low) is based on emissions.

Here we can see that for the high group, agricultural score is remaining approximately constant while emissions are clearly increasing. For the low group, we can also see that agricultural score is increasing while emissions are remaining relatively constant.

This further enforces the realization that more emissions does not always mean better agriculture - an important fact to realize considering the necessity of reducing global emissions.

Fertilizer Use and Emissions: A Strong Link?

Although there are similarities, we can see that the link between emissions and yield is slightly different than the link between fertilizer and yield. This brings to question the nature of the relationship between fertilizer and yield. The plot to the left shows the average fertilizer use and emissions for each country between 2002 and 2016. We can see that the relationship between fertilizer use and emissions is indeed quite linear particularly at low levels - however beyond approximately 50kg/ha, the points begin deviating - some with lower and some with higher emissions than what might be predicted if we assumed a linear relationship. .


Let's take a closer look:

To go deeper, we can also investigate the level of correlation between emissions and fertilizer use. Correlation between the evolution of fertilizer use and emissions between 2002 and 2016 was determined based on Pearson's correlation coefficient which tests for linear relationships. The correlation is plotted against fertilizer use for each country and the plots are coloured based on the emissions levels. It can be seen that for lower fertilizer and emissions use a wide range of both positive and negative correlations are possible, whereas as fertilizer application increases, the correlation is more likely to be positive.

This may indicate that the more fertilizer we use, the more likely we are to have high emissions. In fact, the increase in emissions for a given fertilizer use is generally expected to increase for higher fertilizer use, as more nitrogen is applied than the plants and soil can effectively use. This further enforces the drawbacks of fertilizer use. However, it can also be seen that in general there is a wide spread of correlations even when fertilizer use is similar - which may be explained by the fact that emissions can also depend on the method of fertilizer application, among other factors. .

What does this mean?

We have seen that the link between yield and emissions is not necessarily increasing, however emissions continue to increase. What does this tell us if we consider the CO2 efficiency of our agricultural production?

How is emission intensity of fertilizer use evolving?

Considering the non-increasing relationshp observed between yield and fertilizer emissions, an "emissions efficiency" factor of agriculture was calculated, taking a country's yield indicator divided by their emissions factor. As can be seen in the plot, there is generally a downward trend, indicating that our food production is actually becoming less efficient in terms of emissions, at a time when emissions reduction targets are of key importance.

Is all hope lost?

Although the situation presented thus far is not very optimistic, at the local level there are some improvements being made - as we can see looking at the efficiency evolution of Spain, which is an important agricultural producer. (Whereas Switzerland more closely follows the steadily decreasing global average)

However, overall, it is evident that there is a need for action in order to ensure we can continue feeding earth's growing population without continuously increasing the rate at which we degrade our earth - and at any rate, these methods are becoming less effective, and cannot be expected to support us forever.

Need for change

Overall, those results might be a sign of the soil degradation: due to over-exploitation of soils by intensive agriculture, they’re becoming less and less efficient, even when we’re trying to get high yields back using even more fertilizers that we did before.
Otherwise, this might show that in some countries, we’re using even more fertilizers than the plants can absorb. Thus, even if the soil degradation doesn’t happen, there is no point in using that much fertilizers, plants cannot uptake them. That is thus a waste of money, but also a terrible issue for the planet, as we’ll see right now!

If those results are confirmed by other data sets and analyses, they could be used to support measures aiming to reduce fertilizer use, or to support a transition of agriculture from post-Green Revolution methods to new sustainable ones.
