Evolution of Agriculture
Inputs, Environmental Impacts & Outputs

World population is growing as never before and it is no secret. This increase has been made possible thanks to the progress of agricultural technologies. Modern agriculture utilizes useful tools such as machinery, fertilizers, chemical pesticides. This has made is possible for humans to harvest larger areas, increase efficiency, deal with illnesses and parasites, keep improve food shelf life. These revolutions have drastically changed agricultural practices.


Progress often comes with drawbacks & modern agriculture is not exempt

One drawback is the increase in greenhouse gas emissions. On the following graph, one can see the evolution of the global population (in terms of number of people) and the evolution of gas emissions due to agriculture (in terms of GgCO2).

We can see that greenhouse gas emissions due to agriculture are roughly increasing collinearly with the global population, as agriculture intensifies to continue feeding this growing population. This brings concern for the future, as we are already seeing the environmental consequences of this way of life.

Back to the last decades

Let’s first focus on the crops yield. For these products, the FAO database proposes a consistent measure, which is the ratio between the production of a country and its harvested area. We will refer to this measure as the yield of a country. The following map represents the yield of cereals all over the world between 1961 and 2017. The countries that are displayed in grey are those for which no value is present in the FAO dataset.

If you're interested, you can see the evolution of yield and production for other food types here.


A yield explosion

These plots are more than convincing: the average yield of the top 10 countries has increased threefold between 1961 and 2017. This proves that modern agriculture has improved yields. Moreover, the top 10 yields are composed of different countries in 1961 and in 2017, and a lot of these countries are rather small, like the islands of Saint Vincent and the Grenadines, Belgium or Oman. The USA are only ninth! Of course, a big production isn’t correlated with a big yield, and in 2017, the USA production of cereals is 4,000,000 tonnes whereas the Oman one is only 60000. But those figures show that a small country can be more than 2 times more efficient than the USA with its available area.

It is no big surprise that the yield of food produced per area has skyrocketing over the last 50 years. The Third Agricultural Revolution seemes to have been a big success! It has drastically changed the agriculture habits, and led to an assertion nearly as admitted as “eat your soup, you’ll grow up soon”, which is “the more fertilizer you use, the faster your crops will grow”. But is this sustainable? Probably not. Can we keep going with this method? Probably not. But does data support those intuitions? Do we need to change our mind and practices now, or do we still have time?

Fertilizers still enhanced yield at a global scale

We used a statistical method (Principal Component Analysis, see methods for more) to obtain one variable, called “Agriculture score”, that best represents all the yield of one country. We computed this value for all the countries and colorized it, a posteriori, using the fertilizer use of the country.

The relationship is pretty obvious, right? The green countries, using lower fertilizer amounts, have lower agriculture scores than the blue ones and the red ones (see methods for significance). This relationship can be modeled by a linear relationship and the slope of this curve corresponds to the global effect of fertilizer on the agriculture score.

Overall, fertilizers have enhanced yields at a global scale. Indeed, they played a crucial role in the agricultural revolution. So nothing crazy for now…

Do we need to change our practices?

But what if we take a look at the evolution of this effect over time (i.e. the evolution of the slope of the linear relationship)? Are fertilizers still increasing yields? Or should we focus on alternatives now?

Ok, now that’s interesting: while the median of agriculture score for all countries (left panel, in black) is stable, the median of the fertilizer use is increasing (right panel, in black). This result is even more visible for the high fertilizer group (in red): the agriculture score is slightly decreasing while the fertilizer use continues increasing. Isn’t that crazy? The fertilizer use is increasing, especially in countries that use already higher amounts of fertilizers by hectare, and the agriculture score aren’t increasing much, or even slightly decreasing in high fertilizer countries. Maybe it really is time to change our ways...


And What about the Environment?

Besides helping food to grow (or maybe not, as the data shows), the use of fertilizers also has a significant environmental impact in terms of emissions, as was seen in the first plot of this page. These emissions use occur due to emissions of nitrous oxide when nitrogen is applied to soils. One tonne of nitrous oxide emissions is equivalent to almost 300 tonnes of CO2 emissions - therefore this has important consequences for global warming.

Due to the supposed link between fertilizers and yield, one might assume that in order to achieve high yields, high emissions are necessary or inevitable. But like with fertilizers, we can question whether this really the case? And does this have limits?

Yield and Emissions: inevitable or avoidable?

Principle component analysis was used again to compare agriculture score to emissions due to fertilizer for various countries between 2002 and 2017. As can be seen in the plot, there is a noticeable separation between the countries with high emissions (red), medium emissions (yellow), and low emissions(green), and we can see that countries with the highest agricultural scores are all medium or high emitting countries.

However, looking closer, there are also many countries which have a similar agricultural score, while having significantly different emissions. So... apparently the link between agriculture score and emissions is not as evident as it may seem.

Do we really need to emit to eat?

To go further, we again looked at the evolution of agricultural score and emissions over the years per country. This time the colours (high = red, medium = blue, low = yellow) are based on emissions.

Here it gets interesting again - we can see that for the high group, agricultural score remains approximately constant while emissions are clearly increasing. For the low group, we can also see that agricultural score is increasing while emissions are remaining relatively constant.

So, here we can clearly see that more emissions does not always mean better agriculture - an important realization the necessity of reducing global emissions while continuing to increase production in future years.

Fertilizer Use and Emissions: A Strong Link?

After considering the effect of both fertilizer use and emissions on agricultural score, one may also question the relationship between fertilizer use and emissions directly.

This plot shows the average fertilizer use and emissions for various country between 2002 and 2016. We can see that the relationship between fertilizer use and emissions is indeed quite linear, as we would expect since the emissions are in fact a result of fertilizer use. However, it is interesting to note that beyond approximately 50kg/ha, the points begin deviating, and there are some lower and some with higher emissions than what would be predicted based on a linear relationship. .


Multiple factors at play:

To go deeper, we investigated the level of correlation between emissions and fertilizer use. Correlation between the evolution of fertilizer use and emissions on Pearson's correlation coefficient which tests for linear relationships. The correlation is plotted against fertilizer use for each country and the plots are coloured based on the emissions levels (red = high, yellow = medium, green = low). It can be seen that for lower fertilizer and emissions use a wide range of both positive and negative correlations are possible, whereas as fertilizer application increases, the correlation is more likely to be positive.

This may indicate that the more fertilizer we use, the more likely we are to have high emissions. Moreover, it also shows that there are clearly other factors influencing fertilizer emissions beyond just fertilizer application....

In fact, the increase in emissions for a given fertilizer use is generally expected to increase for higher fertilizer use, as more nitrogen is applied than the plants and soil can effectively use. However, emissions can also depend on the method of fertilizer application, among other factors. .

What does all this mean?

We have seen that the link between yield and fertilizer use, as well as emissions from fertilizer, is no longer increasing. Despite this, fertilizer use and emissions continue to increase. What does this tell us if we consider the efficiency and the environmental impact of our agricultural production?

How environmentally efficient is our agriculture?

To look at this relationship, an "emissions efficiency" factor was calculated, which takes a country's agricultural score divided by their emissions factor, to give an idea of the emissions intensity of their agriculture. As can be seen in the plot, there is generally a downward trend... we actually need to emit more CO2 for a given agricultural output, at a time when emissions reduction targets are of key importance.

Is all hope lost?

At least at the local level, there are some improvements being made - as we can see looking at the emissions efficiency evolution for Spain, an important agricultural producer. (Whereas Switzerland more closely follows the steadily decreasing global average)

However, overall, it is evident that there is a need for action in order to ensure we can continue feeding earth's growing population without continuously increasing the rate at which we degrade our earth - and at any rate, these methods are becoming less effective, and cannot be expected to support us forever.

Need for change

What can we say from these results?

As we have seen, the effectiveness of fertilizer is decreasing. Overall, this may be a sign of soil degradation: due to over-exploitation of soils by intensive agriculture, they are becoming less and less efficient, even when we’re trying to get high yields by using more fertilizers than ever before.

This may also show that in some countries, we’re using even more fertilizers than the plants can absorb. Thus, even if the soil degradation doesn’t happen, there is no point in using that much fertilizers, as plants cannot use it. That is thus a waste of money, and also a terrible issue for the planet, given the emissions associated with fertilizer use (as well as other pollutants).

We can also wee that while agriculture is highly emitting, these high emissions are not necessarily resulting in higher yields, which makes us question why we do not make more efforts to increase efficiency and decrease emissions in this sector.

If these results are confirmed by other data sets and analyses, this can be strong evidence to support measures aiming to support an agricultural transition away from the current, environmentally intensive methods.
